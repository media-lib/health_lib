    I Was Perfectly Healthy Until


    "I was perfectly healthy until.."

    "..until I was in that car accident"
    "..until I fell and broke a bone and then everything went bad"
    "..until my husband died, then I never regained my health."
    "..until I lost my job."

    What's the trigger between stressful events and unexplained, prolonged, declining health?

    Few recognize that humans have a built-in defense mechanism that can work in reverse. When faced with a sudden threat to life, such as a ferocious animal let's say, we have to have instant energy to flee from it. A perceived threat instantly thrusts stress hormones from the adrenal glands into our blood stream which immediately increases blood sugar and circulating fat levels, giving us a tremendous, albeit temporary, boost of energy. The heart pounds to rush the hormones throughout the system. For a brief moment or two, we have enough energy to shinny up a tree and remove ourselves from the threatening situation. Under normal circumstances, we wouldn't have the ability to leap into a tree to avoid danger.

    The problem is, when we are under chronic stress, we can trigger this defensive mechanism. Cholesterol and blood sugar levels rise. As the adrenal glands, located on top of the kidneys, secrete stress hormones, other hormones are unbalanced. The onset of hormonally-related diseases (like breast and prostate cancer) may eventually follow.

    Examine some of the reports linking stress to high cholesterol and blood sugar.

    In one documented study, the total cholesterol in one subject increased a whopping 145 points, from 150 to 295, due to mental stress. [Circulation 19: April 1959]

    A paper written 38 years ago in the Journal of the American Medical Association analyzed psychological factors among 25 newly-diagnosed diabetics. Researchers found many were depressed, had recently lost a loved one, had faced financial losses or loss of employment. Many well-controlled diabetics, when faced with a loss or stressful event, can no longer control their blood sugar. Researchers noted the case of a policeman with a 4-year history of well-controlled diabetes. When told his 15-year old daughter had leukemia, his blood sugar was uncontrollable. When further blood tests revealed his daughter did not have leukemia and the diagnosis was mistaken, his blood sugar dropped and could again be controlled with insulin. (Journal American Medical Assn, July 20, 1963] A more recent study revealed that adults undergoing physical or emotional stress are 60 percent more likely to be diagnosed with diabetes. [Diabetes Care 23: 197-201, 2000] Insulin-resistance, the inability of insulin to enter cells to create energy, is related to stress and it is known as a causal factor in prostate and breast cancer. [European Urology 39: 151-58, 2001]

    Briefly examine some of the other links between stress and disease:

    Stress interferes with estrogen production in females and can increase their risk of heart disease. [Wake Forest Univ, March 9, 2001]

    Even though a bacterium (Helicobacter pylori) has been identified as the organism responsible for gastric ulcers, the link between stress and ulcers is longstanding. Half of the population is infected with H. pylori but only a few develop ulcers. The mucus layer of the digestive tract buffers against disease, but stress can interfere with mucus production. [Journal American Medical Assn 237: 199, 1999]

    Not all people who are infected with rhinovirus (the virus that causes colds) actually develop symptoms. When 17 adults were purposely infected with the cold virus only 12 developed symptoms. Physical or emotional events were more common among those who developed symptoms. [Behavioral Medicine 18: 115-20, 1992]

    Men who chronically worry over financial, social or health conditions are more than twice as likely to develop coronary heart disease as men who are not chronic worriers. [Circulation 95: 818-24, 1997]

    Herpes infections (genital and oral) are more likely to flare up during stress. [Psychotherapy Psychosomatics 66: 3-26, 1997; Journal Behavioral Medicine 8: 249-60, 1985]

    Severe stress increases the risk of a relapse of breast cancer by six times. [Lancet 346: 1194-96, 1995]

    Stress negatively affects wound healing. [Lancet 346: 1194-96, 1995]

    Among people undergoing treatment for angina (chest pain), 40 percent overall experienced angina following treatment, but this figure rose to 90 percent among highly-stressed individuals. In other words, stress over-rode the effect of medications. [Psychosomatic Medicine 61: 834-41, 1999

    The problem: cortisol, an adrenal hormone

    Researchers have identified cortisol, a hormone secreted by the adrenal glands, as problematic in stress-related disease. Take a look at some of the data linking cortisol with health problems:

    Elevated cortisol levels impair memory. [Life Science 58: 475-83, 1996]

    High cortisol levels are associated with Alzheimer's disease. [Biological Psychiatry 42: 1030-38, 1997]

    Cortisol stunts the immune system. [Science News 152: 350, 1997]

    Patients with various forms of retinal disease (retinitis pigmentosa) have elevated cortisol levels. [Ophthalmology times, March 15, 1998]

    The level of cortisol increases by 3 times among patients undergoing surgery. [Journal Endocrinological Investigation 21: 12-19, 1998]

    How to counter cortisol and stress nutritionally

    The adrenal glands use up about a third of the vitamin C in the body. Vitamin C appears to be the counter agent to cortisol, one of the major stress hormones produced by the adrenal glands. [Z Ernahrungswiss 24: 219-30, 1985] As cortisol is released from the adrenal glands, vitamin C levels also drop in the adrenals. [International Journal Vitamin Nutrition Research 52: 176-85, 1982] In an animal study, the provision of high-dose vitamin C "essentially abolished the secretion of stress hormones" says a University of Alabama report released in 1999. [CNN August 23, 1999] During periods of high stress, adults may need to take 1000-2000 milligrams of vitamin C every 4 hours, preferably with bioflavonoids (citrus, grape seed, cranberry, bilberry, quercetin) that help maintain high vitamin C levels.

    Vitamin A (2500-5000 IU), ginkgo biloba (100-200 mg), acetyl L-carnitine (1500 mg) are other food supplements that have been shown to counter the adverse effects of cortisol. [Drug Topics, December 8, 1997]

    Because stress hormones block the conversion in the liver of essential omega-3 fats into DHA (docosahaenoic acid) which lines nerve cells throughout the body, it is important for individuals undergoing prolonged stress to consume fish-liver oil that has already been converted into DHA. Look for fish oil products that provide 1000-2000 mg of DHA per day.

    Increase laughter

    Laughter is good medicine. The Bible says "A merry heart doeth good like a medicine." --King Solomon. Laughter decreases the production of cortisol. [Clinical Research 36: 435A, 1988] Men who viewed a humorous videotape were shown to have lower levels of stress hormones in an experimental study. [FASEB Journal 2: A1570, 1988] So rent a Laurel and Hardy videotape, and laugh away. We can't avoid stress, but can learn to control it effects upon the mind and body
